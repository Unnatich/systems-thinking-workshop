Foundations

What is Systems Thinking?

Its a way of looking at the world as a series of interconnected webs, very detailed nuanced web, within webs within webs and all of them interacting with each other in inter-esting and counter-intuitive ways

It is the way of looking at the world moving out of the PERSONAL Perspective and looking at it at a system level - like how governments work

PM Example

Instagram's Explore Page: Webs Within Webs

The Explore page isn't just an algorithm. It's a system of user behavior, content creator incentives, ad revenue goals, content moderation policies, network effects, and cultural trends — all interacting.

When Instagram pushed Reels to compete with TikTok, it changed creator behavior, which changed user engagement patterns, which changed what brands were willing to pay for, which shifted the entire content ecosystem. One product decision, cascading through interconnected webs.

PM Takeaway: A single feature change doesn't land in isolation — it ripples through every layer of the system.

PM Example

Slack as a Self-Organizing System

Slack didn't design how teams use it — teams did. Channels, emoji reactions as approval workflows, threads becoming standup replacements, bots for deployment — none of this was planned.

The product became an organism where user behavior shaped the system more than the product team did. The PM's job was to observe the emergent behavior and build for it, not dictate it.

PM Takeaway: The best products become self-organizing systems — your job is to observe and amplify, not control.

Principles of Systems Thinking

Principle 01
01

Problems Are Systemic, Not Personal

Poverty - how long people have tried to eradicate poverty but failed?

Obesity, crime, unemployment, corruption, depression, spam — these persist not because individuals are failing, but because the systems producing them are intact.

PM Example

Why Users Churn: It's Never Just One Thing

A PM sees churn spike and blames the last feature release. But churn is systemic: onboarding confusion 3 months ago → user never discovered core value → support ticket went unanswered → competitor ran a targeted ad → user left.

Blaming the last feature is personal thinking. Mapping the full journey is systems thinking.

PM Takeaway: Churn is a systemic problem. Trace the full causal chain, not just the last touch.

02

See Everything as a System & Embrace Counter-Intuition

If you understand these principles - then only you will become a systems thinker

Systems are counter-intuitive - no direct variable co-relations exist, you can break into sub-systems and see the relations and make intelligent guesses

Principle 03
03

Non-Linearity: Chaotic, Not Random

A non-linear system is not random but chaotic i.e weather system

Real life scenarios are not simple algebraic systems - simple systems is monopoly, chess

Your strategy might become redundant soon

Principle 03 — continued

Good Intentions ≠ Good Outcomes

A non linear system also means if you think just because you have good intentions - the outcome is good nopes - it means nothing

Simple minded people make changes and get opposite results

PM Example

YouTube's Recommendation Engine & Radicalization

YouTube optimized for watch time with good intentions — give users more of what they enjoy. But the non-linear effect: the algorithm nudged users toward increasingly extreme content because extreme content held attention longer.

A product metric optimized in isolation created societal-scale unintended consequences. Good intention, deeply problematic outcome.

PM Takeaway: Optimizing a single metric in a non-linear system can create outcomes you never modeled for.
Principle 04
04

Local Actions → Global Impact & Systems Backfire

Local actions have global impact i.e culture precedent - my company is one node in a large system

A system can backfire on itself

The single greatest enemy of the system is the system itself haha! i.e A simple example is terrorism

PM Example

Flipkart's Big Billion Day Backfires

Flipkart's first Big Billion Day sale (2014) — aggressive discounting to capture market share. Local action: slash prices. Systemic result: site crashed, orders got cancelled, sellers were furious about margin compression, customer trust dropped, and the media narrative flipped from "amazing sale" to "disaster."

A company is one node in a system of sellers, logistics, servers, customer expectations, and media. The system backfired.

PM Takeaway: Your product doesn't exist in a vacuum — sellers, infra, media, and users form the system around it.
Principle 05
05

Balance & Harmony — No Knee-Jerk Reactions

Systems are about balance and harmony - knee jerk reactions never work in a complex systems i.e Choosing a leader, choosing growth, choosing personal growth

PM Example

Uber's Surge Pricing Backlash

Uber's surge pricing is a balancing mechanism — match supply and demand. But during emergencies, surge prices spiked dramatically. Public outrage followed.

Uber's knee-jerk response: cap surge during emergencies. But that broke the balancing loop — drivers had no incentive to come online during dangerous conditions, leading to zero availability. Systems require balance, not reactive fixes. Uber eventually landed on a nuanced middle ground.

PM Takeaway: Knee-jerk policy changes can break the very mechanism that makes your system work.
Principle 06
06

Being aware of false boundaries, redrawing the boundaries can give me a better system i.e remove bad actors

The universe is dynamic and not static i.e ergo reductionism - you are yourself a dynamic system

Principle 07
07

Value Complexity, Think Long-Term

Systems thinkers value complexity and nuance and hence value life long learning

Systems thinkers see the value to build a system in understanding the system or discovery

Expecting unforeseen consequences for your actions - long term thinking - forward looking

Systems thinkers are great long term thinkers and be prepared for chaos

Principle 08
08

Don't Jump to Solution Space

Systems thinking bank on the fact that systems dwarf them so they should self-reflect a lot and hence don't jump to solution space directly

As systems cause their own problems

PM Example

Google Wave: Solving Without Understanding

Google Wave was a technically brilliant product that combined email, messaging, and collaboration. The team jumped to solution space without deeply understanding how people actually communicate.

They built for how communication should work in theory, not how the system of human communication actually behaves. It launched in 2009 and was shut down within a year.

PM Takeaway: Spend more time in discovery than in solution. Understand the system before you try to redesign it.

Principle 12
12

Feedback Loops, Patterns & Multiple Perspectives

Integrating multiple perspectives

Studying pattern and cycles

Systems thinkers mostly know most problems are because of poor feedback loops and delays in good feedback loops

PM Example

Zomato Ratings: The Feedback Loop That Makes or Breaks Restaurants

A restaurant gets a few poor ratings early on → fewer orders → less revenue → corners cut on quality → worse ratings. That's a reinforcing loop spiraling downward.

Meanwhile, a genuinely great new restaurant takes months to accumulate enough reviews to surface in search — a delay in the positive feedback loop. By the time the algorithm catches up, the restaurant may have shut down.

PM Takeaway: When you design rating systems, you're designing feedback loops. Most product problems trace back to poor or delayed feedback.
